About

Twenty years of building at the frontier

Ylab was founded by a technology executive who has spent his career building organizations and communities at the intersection of technology and society.

The story

Some careers are organized around a single institution or sector. This one has been organized around a question: how do you build organizations that can actually change important things?

That question has led across sectors — from enterprise software to healthcare technology, civic platforms to education, government modernization to global development — and across geographies, with deep ties to communities in North America, Europe, and Africa. The connecting thread has always been institution building: creating the teams, structures, cultures, and ecosystems that allow important work to happen at scale.

The early chapters were in enterprise technology, learning how software scales, how engineering teams work, and what separates organizations that ship from those that don't. That foundation proved essential for everything that followed.

In healthcare, the work was about building technology infrastructure that could become a durable part of the clinical system — technology that a doctor would trust in a high-stakes moment, embedded in workflows, compliant, safe, and actually better than what existed before. That experience created a lasting conviction that the hardest problems in health technology are organizational and regulatory, not computational.

In civic technology, the work touched elections, government services, and democratic participation. Working alongside civil servants, policy advocates, and community organizers made it clear how much context matters — and how easily well-intentioned technology projects can misfire when they're disconnected from the institutional realities they're trying to change.

Education technology came next, with work focused on connecting people to economic opportunity through learning and credentialing. The most important lesson: motivation and belonging matter more than content delivery. The hard problem of education is not information — it is creating environments where people grow.

Alongside operating roles, there has been consistent advisory work, including advising major international institutions on technology strategy — work that requires translating between the pace of technical change and the rhythms of large, complex organizations with global mandates. Teaching at UC Berkeley offered a different kind of contribution: engaging the next generation of leaders with the hard questions about technology, power, and responsibility.

Research and publishing have been a constant. Case studies, conference talks, written essays, and experiments in public thinking have been how ideas get tested, refined, and shared — and how communities of practice get built.

Ylab is the institutional expression of all of this: a platform for working on important problems, supporting exceptional people, and contributing to the creation of new institutions that are fit for the AI era.
